Description

The Cabbage White Butterfly (Pieris rapae) is a charming spectacle with closed wings, resembling a smaller version of the Pieris brassicae. Its topside is a delightful creamy white adorned with subtle black tips on the forewings. Females showcase an extra touch with two black spots at the center of their forewings. Flip to the underside, and you'll find a soft yellow canvas sprinkled with dainty black speckles. Mistaken for a moth due to its unassuming appearance, this butterfly unveils a wingspan ranging from 32–47 mm (1.3–1.9 in). In flight, Pieris rapae exhibits a graceful wingbeat, fluttering about 12.8 times per second. Explore the simple yet captivating beauty of this butterfly, where elegance meets subtlety in every delicate detail.

Habitats

Small Whites, scientifically known as P. rapae, engage in a fascinating dance of life across Europe, Asia, and North Africa. Accidentally introduced to Quebec in 1860, they swiftly conquered North America, spreading from Lower Austral to Canada.

A single female can be the matriarch of millions in just a few generations. In the British Isles, they grace the skies twice a year, while in North America, they flutter continuously from spring emergence to fall's frosty embrace.

These delicate creatures prefer diverse plant associations and can be spotted in towns, natural habitats, and even venturing into forest clearings. Distinct subspecies like P. r. rapae in Europe and P. r. crucivora in Asia showcase the evolutionary tapestry of these enchanting butterflies.

Their life cycle, from egg-laying on cabbage family plants to caterpillar feasts on cabbages and pupa transformations, unfolds as a captivating journey in the world of Small Whites. As diurnal creatures, Small Whites cover impressive distances, with females flying about 0.7 km per day. Males tirelessly patrol host plants, contributing to the enchanting spectacle of their mating habits.

Life Cycle

Egg

The small white readily lays eggs on both cultivated and wild members of the cabbage family, such as charlock (Sinapis arvensis) and hedge mustard (Sisymbrium officinale). P. rapae lays eggs singularly on the host plant. The egg is characterized by a yellowish color and 12 longitudinal ridges. Egg production peaks about a week after adulthood, and the female can live up to 3 weeks. Females tend to lay fewer eggs on plants in clumps than on isolated plants. Although there's a suggestion of isothiocyanate compounds in the family Brassicaceae evolving to reduce herbivory, this is not generally accepted due to the small white's immunity to the isothiocyanate forming reaction.

Caterpillar

Traditionally known in the United States as the imported cabbage worm, now more commonly the cabbage white, the caterpillars are bluish-green, with tiny black points, a black ring around the spiracles, and a lateral row of yellow dashes, and a yellow middorsal line. Caterpillars rest on the undersides of the leaves, making them less visible to predators. The larval stage of the small white can be a pest on cultivated cabbages, kale, radish, broccoli, and horseradish. The larva is considered a serious pest for commercial growth of cabbage and other Brassicaceae.

Pupa

The pupa of P. rapae is very similar to that of P. napi. It is brown to mottled-gray or yellowish, matching the background color. It has a large head cone, with a vertical abdomen and flared subdorsal ridge. The pupa of P. rapae and P. napi can be easily distinguished by comparing the proboscis sheath. In P. rapae, the proboscis sheath extends far beyond the antennal sheath, while in P. napi, only a very short distance.

Reproduction

Adult Pieris rapae use a mix of visual and olfactory cues during their foraging flights. They show a preference for vibrant colors like purple, blue, and yellow in flowers. Some flowers even have a UV guide, aiding in the butterfly's nectar search—a crucial aspect of their foraging strategy.

When feeding, adults actively seek out specific flower colors, extending their proboscis before landing. The butterfly identifies flowers through vision and odor, triggered by certain chemical compounds that prompt reflex proboscis extension.

Courtship and reproduction in P. rapae involve captivating behaviors. Males engage in mesmerizing aerial displays upon spotting a female. After landing, the male flutters and spreads his wings, initiating a courtship ritual. Females use mustard oils to locate host plants and lay eggs singly on host leaves.

Mating pairs exhibit unique behaviors, with males benefiting from sodium uptake through mud-puddling, contributing to increased reproductive success. In the northern hemisphere, adults are active from March to October, with variations in wing spots and size between spring and summer adults.
